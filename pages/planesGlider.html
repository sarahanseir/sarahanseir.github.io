Airplanes & Gliders

Airplanes

These aircrafts have fixed wings and are considered heavier then their surronding air. They are able to fly by the lift generated from the special shape of their wings. [1] When air flows over the camber of the wing, the air increases in speed. This causes a decrease in air pressure above the wing. When air flows under the wing, the air decreases in speed. This causes an increase in pressure under the wing. Coupled together, this pressure difference generates most of the plane's lift. The physical efficiency of this aircraft is why it is one of the largest forms of transportation over expansive areas.

Gliders

Like the airplane, a glider is a "heavier-than-air aircraft". However, unlike the airplane, a glider is unpowered. This means another force must help the glider take off, such as gravity or the force from another aircraft. People will fly in gliders for recreational and sport purposes. The physcial structure of a glider is very similar to an airplane and the wings provide lift in virtually the same way. In fact, the development of the glider allowed the Wright Brothers to develop the airplane.[2]
