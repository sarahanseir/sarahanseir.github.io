Rotorcraft & Powered Lift

Rotorcraft

A rotorcraft is a heavier-than-air aircraft that achieves lift via rotating blades. Two examples include the helicoper and a gyrocopter. A helicopter has an engine powered rotor that spins and provides lift for the plane. There is also a rotar at the tail that provides the aircrafts thrust. A gyrocopter has a rotor that is unpowered, and only spins when the surronding air prompts this motion. In order for the gyrocopeter to obtai thrust, the aircraft will have an engine powered propeller.[1][2] The rotors on these aircrafts enable quick vertical movement. However, they lack the horizonta; effiency of airplanes.

Powered Lift

The FAA's 14 CFR 1.1 defines “Powered lift” as a heavier-than-air aircraft that acheives lift from both engine power and fixed wing. This means they have both rotors like a rotorcraft, and wings like an airplane. This enables the aircraft to acheive vertical flexibility that a helocipter experiences, while also benefiting from the horizontal efficency of an airplane. [3] These aircrafts are used by the military since they are easy to land in smaller areas without a runway. This may be on an aircraft carrier or battlefield.
